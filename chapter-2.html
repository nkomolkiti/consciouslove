ABOUT VERBAL ABUSE

Psychological abuse of a child is a pattern of intentional verbal or behavioral actions or lack of actions that convey to a child the message that he or she is worthless, flawed, unloved, unwanted, endangered, or only of value to meet someone else's needs. — Samantha Gluck, a journalist specializing in healthcare trends, mental health, health and fitness

Verbal abuse doesn't have to be loud; it can be quiet. Verbal abuse can be very harmful on children who haven't developed a conscious defense mechanism. The effects on the child can include, but not limited to, anxiety, depression, and self-criticism. Self-criticism is a mental habit of attributing all mistakes to yourself, such as "I failed because I am stupid" or "Nothing good will ever happen to me because I'm not good enough".

Sometimes it can come out as a joke (which is obviously not funny), or as a reflection of one's insecurity. The child might also view it as 'normal' and it becomes the continuation of action.

COMMON TYPES OF VERBAL ABUSE

  • 01 ABUSIVE ANGER

      Any form of yelling and screaming, particularly out of context. Even yelling "Shut up!" is abusive. No one deserves to be yelled at.

  • 02 DISAPPEARING ACT: IGNORED

      Children's information and relationships with the world come to them second-hand. With a caring parent who responds to their cues, they begin to understand that they matter and are worthy of attention; building healthy self-esteem. The children who are ignored instead question their place in the world, even though they don't know why.

  • 03 ABSENCE OF PRAISE & SUPPORT

      People are not psychic and therefore do not know that they're appreciated without communication. Words that articulate why a child is worthy of love and attention are as essential as food, water, clothing, and shelter. They are needed for the child to thrive and develop normally.

  • 04 COMPARING & CRITICIZING

      Being constantly compared to another person can either result in the child being pressured to be better in order to 'satisfy' the parents, or feeling like she is not loved, and becoming less confident in herself. Examples are such as "You did great on your exam but why can't you do that all the time?" and "Can't you be good like your sister?"

  • 05 JOKES

      The parent may say something very upsetting to the child and, after seeing her reaction add, "It was just a joke!" This may result in the young child being insecure about whatever is made as a joke.

  • 06 GASLIGHTING

      This is a form of manipulation that results in the child doubting his or her perceptions. It is a simple statement that says that something that happened didn't. It is very effective on the child because he accepts the adult's words as authority until he gets old enough to begin questioning his parent. This erodes the child's confidence in his own thoughts as children haven't developed conscious defense mechanisms.
      Example: a mother promises a child candy if he's quiet, but when the child asks about the candy afterwards, she goes against her word by stating that she has not promised him anything.

  • 07 BLAMING

      The child gets scolded for something outside of his control, such as "I missed the interview because I have to take care of you." This leaves the child confused from being unfairly accused of something that is not his fault.

  • 08 FORGETTING

      A parent who always forgets promises can result in a child feeling unworthy and angry.

  • 09 UNDERMINING

      Imagine having a parent who undermines whatever the child is trying to do,whether it be deciding her own future, or striving to be best at something so her parents are proud of her. By undermining every action the child takes, it makes her question her own opinions and interests.

  • 10 FOR YOUR OWN GOOD

      Most times, both the loud and the quiet kinds of verbal abuse are rationalized by the need to correct perceived flaws in the child's character or behavior. Hypercriticality (magnifying every misstep or mistake) may be "justified" by having to make sure the child "isn't too full of herself" and other self-serving statements. Delivered in a quiet tone, this barrage of criticism makes a child believe she's unworthy of attention and support because she's worthless.

  • 11 STONEWALLING

      It's hard enough to deal with stonewalling when you're an adult—your partner's refusal to answer that ends up with frustration—but it's absolutely devastating to a child who doesn't have any way of defending herself. The child's self-esteem lowers as they think they aren't good enough to warrant their parents' attention.

  • 12 THREATENING

      Threatening like throwing a child out of the house, or saying that people will hate him if he doesn't follow the parent's advice, create a sense that the child is not wanted.

"No discussion of verbal abuse would be complete without including the absence of word as a form of abuse."
— Dr. Gregory Jantz

WHAT'S THE IMPACT?

The impact of verbal aggression and abuse tends to be discounted and marginalized; there seems to be an unspoken agreement that such abuse is "only words," as people cite the children's rhyme: "Sticks and stones may break my bones, but words shall never hurt me." This is not that people are not aware of verbal abuse, but rather there is not enough discussion about it.

The positive clearly does not offset the negative. This means even if there are one loving parent and one abusive parent, words are still damaging. Science backs up that repetitive verbal abuse can lead to the psychological impact such as having low self-esteem, anxeity, and can affect the development of brains. Many of those experienced verbal abuse in their childhood can still hear negative voices in their head, and it takes a long time to rid of this mental habit and realize that it is not the reality of the situation. It poisons the family relationship, in which is the most essential to children's fundamental development in life.
